Prototype Writeup

What have we done so far?

We built a working web prototype for F1 Sonic Lap that loads Formula 1 telemetry from the 2024 Belgian Grand Prix qualifying session and displays it across four synchronized visualizations. The telemetry player renders RPM, throttle, brake, and gear as stacked D3 charts with a shared interactive playhead that responds to hover and click. A Tone.js audio engine maps RPM to oscillator pitch (110–440 Hz), throttle to output volume, and braking to a low-frequency rumble so the viewer can literally hear each driver working through Spa-Francorchamps. Playback buttons for Verstappen and Perez let users switch between the two laps mid-session. An interactive track minimap colours the Spa circuit by speed and animates a dot that tracks the audio playhead in real time. A smoothness-versus-lap-time scatter plot shows all 20 qualifying drivers, with VER and PER highlighted, previewing the central statistical claim of the finished project. A delta chart shows the cumulative time gap around the lap, identifying the corners where Verstappen builds his advantage. All charts are linked through a shared time index so any interaction propagates across the page simultaneously.

What will be the most challenging part to design and why?

The most challenging aspect will be making the audio feel analytically meaningful rather than merely decorative. The sonification must teach the viewer something precise about driver behaviour, specifically that Verstappen's inputs are smoother and more deliberate while remaining perceptually intuitive to someone with no musical training. Achieving this requires carefully calibrated mapping functions: too compressed a pitch range makes lap differences inaudible, while too wide a range sounds unpleasant. Synchronisation is also a hard problem because Tone.js schedules audio on its own transport clock, which can drift relative to the D3 animation frame loop, causing the minimap dot and the telemetry playhead to lag behind the sound. The final version will need a phase-locked loop strategy to keep visual and audio clocks aligned. Beyond the technical challenge, the narrative arc must guide a first-time viewer from hearing the difference, to seeing the difference in the charts, to understanding why smoothness produces speed — all without a wall of explanatory text. Annotations, progressive disclosure, and a well-designed blind-test interaction will be the key design levers for the next iteration.
